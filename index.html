Started College

In August 2011 I started pursuing a B.S. degree in Computer Science at Purdue University Fort Wayne. Prior to college, I had very minimal experience with coding. I would play with the markup on my MySpace page and sometimes tinker with a website's source code, but nothing that would qualify as programming. My first encounter with programming was my CS 160 course at Purdue. It was very overwhelming and intimidating at first and I worked a lot with the teaching assisstant to get the basics down, but once I did everything just clicked. It was as if I was in a dark room full of doors and opening them one at a time to let light in. Then finally I found a way to open all the doors at once and I could finally see how everything worked together. It was this feeling that hooked me. I was determined to learn more.

Medical Informatics Engineering

After gaining enough knowledge from my first two years of school, I got tired of waiting tables and decided to apply for a software job. I got a call back from Medical Informatics Engineering (MIE) and got hired directly after the interview for a part-time position. I was officially a part of the IT field. I worked on code for the WebChart and NoMoreClipboard projects. These are cloud based EMR/EHR systems built for physician, patient, and enterprise use. I specifically worked with data imports involving string parsing data in JSON and XML, AJAX requests, and user input. I also updated the architecture and design of the application's system settings to be more accessible and manageable in the UI using JavaScript/jQuery, CSS, and HTML. I gained a lot work experience here specifically in C, jQuery, Git, and MySQL.

Android Malware Research Project

While still attending school and working at MIE, I was asked to participate in a research project with three other students led by one of my professors, Dr. Britton Wolfe. I accepted gratefully and was very honored since I admired Dr. Wolfe and he was one of my favorite professors. The project was to research Android Malware. The idea was to take an Android app and be able to tell if it was malicious or not. We split up into two groups testing two different methodologies for differentiating the data. This research led to Dr. Wolfe contributing to two publications, "High Precision Screening for Android Malware with Dimensionality Reduction," and "Comprehensive Behavior Profiling for Proactive Android Malware Detection."

WoundView

My senior year I worked on my Capstone project, WoundView, leading a team of four and working with Purdue University and Parkview Mirro Center for Research and Innovation. This was a full year project with research done in the Summer, documentation written in the Fall and Winter, and code implemented in the Spring. The goal of the project was to improve the current standard of wound assessment.

We did research in photogrammetry technology to be able to make measurements of length and width of a wound via an image. We then took this research and created a web application that implemented it using OpenCV, Python, Flask, and Javscript/jQuery. This was where I really dived into the infrastructure behind a website since I needed to set up my own server using Ubuntu 14 Trusty Tahr, Nginx, and a free DNS service. I implemented all of this during the Winter and then moved to the cloud in the Spring using AWS's free tier.

Graduated from Purdue University

After five strenuous years of hard work, I had finally accomplished all of the requirements needed to receive a B.S. in Computer Science and a minor in Mathematics. I was officially a graduate and ready to take on the next step.

Our Sunday Visitor

After graduating, I sent out many applications to get a full-time position. I had several interviews and finally made the decision to work full-time as an Associate Software Developer at Our Sunday Visitor (OSV). The first week or so was used to train and learn their tech stack which consisted of Ruby on Rails, React, and PostgreSQL. I was able to quickly ramp up and start working on defects. It wasn't long before I started knocking out my own features. My manager and others saw my progress and offered me a promotion to Software Developer after only a year. I worked with both on-site and remote workers developing what would be the beginning of OSV Hub.

After about a year of that, OSV decided to redesign OSV Hub with a Microsoft stack. We switched to a .NET Core back-end and an Angular front-end. I trained and was able to quickly learn on the job once again. We were running at a status quo for some time, so I started volunteering when ever possible to help out and gain some responsibility. My manager recognized this and asked me to be the new Scrum Master. I delightfully accepted and have been managing the team processes since.

Another opportunity arose when the developers responsible for the OSV Hub release process were pulled away to other requirements and they asked for volunteers to take over. I immediately stepped up since I had been interested in learning more about the release process for some time and it was another way to gain more experience. Since then I've been carrying out the day to day processes and still continue to look for any new opportunities as they come.
